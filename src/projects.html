Pyramids of Giza

The last surviving of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, the Pyramids of Giza are one of the most recognizable landmarks. But who built them and Why!??

The Ancient Egyptians built the pyramids as tombs to bury their mighty noble Pharaohs accompanied with all burial material goods that ranged from everyday objects like personal belongings to the most expensive items like Jewelry. They had a strong belief in the afterlife and that the dead kings would use these items.

Guarding these mortuary buildings is the “Sphinx”, one of the ancient world’s iconic monuments. Having the face of a pharaoh and the body of a lion it is considered the largest monolith statue in the world.


QARUN LAKE

Located in Fayoum City, Lake Qarun can be found which happens to be the third largest lake in Egypt.

The lake has an interesting history and there are some fascinating theories about how it came to be known as Lake Qarun and it remains a mystery how it was formed.

Near the lake, an ancient building called Qasr Qarun, or Qarun’s Mansion, is believed to be the house of an opulent exultant individual named “Qarun” as described and mentioned in both the Bible and the Quran who was a contemporary of Prophet Moses. The story tells that all his mansions and possessions flooded and swallowed up in the earth as a punishment from God for his sins.
There are many myths and lots of stories but one thing is clear: it is beautiful

Karnak Temple and the Valley of the Kings

The Karnak Temple located on the banks of the River Nile in a small village at Al-Karnak, north of Luxor City in Upper Egypt is a fascinating colossal open-air museum and the largest ancient religious site ever built.

Consisting of three main temples with colorful wall art of tombs and great stone columns in the temples, is what make it the second most important historical place of Egypt just after the Giza Pyramids near Cairo.

Hidden between rocky escarpments and behind the cliffs lies the famous Valley of the Kings. Within the valley are 63 tombs that are a roll-call of famous names of Egyptian history including the famous boy-king Tutankhamun. The royal tombs are decorated with wonderfully vivid wall paintings and scenes from Egyptian mythology that give clues to the beliefs and funerary rituals back then.


St. Catherine's Monastery

One of the oldest monasteries in the world, St. Catherine's stands at the foot of Mount Sinai, where Moses is said to have received the Ten Commandments when god spoke to him. This desert monastery is home to an incredible collection of religious iconography, art, and manuscripts as well as the home of the burning bush.

It was built by the Byzantine Emperor Justinian in the 6th century AD and the area is sacred to all three monotheistic religions, Judaism, Christianity and Islam. In the walled compound there is a Fatimid mosque built next to the Orthodox Church, a rare coexistence of religions in today’s World.

Roman Amphitheatre

The Roman Amphitheatre is one of the most popular monuments located in the heart of the city of Alexandria, the second most important city in Egypt, after Cairo, the capital of the country.

It is a large circular small Roman theatre referred to as an Amphitheatre and it is the only one of its type to be found in the country.

Amphitheaters were quite spread during the reign of the Romans in different countries like Greece, Italy, and Turkey over a large empire with many examples of these structures still present in many regions around Europe and the Middle East.

Off of the theatre portico, you can find also several auditoriums (auditoria), small lecture halls with stone seating of various design, dating to the 5th and 6th centuries AD.

Egyptian culture has thousands of years of recorded history. It passed by many diverse civilizations from Ancient Egypt to the Egypt we know now. It was able to maintain a stable and complex culture throughout the years which later on influenced other cultures like the European culture, Middle East Culture, and the African culture. Their mother tongue is Arabic and most Egyptians are quiet intelligent when it comes to knowing other languages so you’d find a little bit of English here and a few French over there. Yeah, Egyptians are flexible like that!

Egyptian people are one of a kind. They are well known for their hospitality and kindness. You’d never feel like a stranger when you’re among Egyptians. Egyptians also have a huge respect for their families and consider them as one of the most valuable aspects in their lives. When you meet an Egyptian, always greet them with “Al-Salam Aleikum” and a hand shake as a sign of respect. When an Egyptian invites you over for Dinner, bring them a symbolic gift such as a box of chocolate. It shows that you’re grateful for the invitation. When it comes to communication, Egyptians can be a little, just a little, “Loud” and they use a lot of hand gestures. It’s an indication that they’re excited or trying to demonstrate a certain point.

Cairo, the “other” city that never sleeps. Cairo the capital of Egypt and is almost awake 24/7. You’d always find open shops, cafes and restaurants anytime of the day. It is full of very unique and fun activities to do. Cairo surely is a unique town.

Egypt, with its very diverse culture and people, has many celebrations across the year. This is due to the diverse religions Egypt hosts (Islam and Christianity) and national celebrations as well. From the very famous and well-celebrated festivities in Egypt are the following:

Eid Al Fetr (Al Fetr Feast)

It comes after the holy month of Ramadan. It is a symbol of breaking Ramadan’s fast and lasts for three days. Egyptian women usually bake a special kind of cookies called “Kahk” in this feast. People celebrate it by buying new clothes, praying together in the early morning of the first day of feast, then visit their families where they spend the day having breakfast and lunch together.

Eid Al Adha (Al-Adha Feast)

During the Hajj time (when the Muslim from all over the world visit the holy city of Mecca-Saudi Arabia to do The Hajj ceremony), Egyptians celebrate this time with Al-Adha Feast for four days. This feast is an Islamic event and usually celebrated by scarifying a sheep or cow for the sake of the less fortunate of the society. Egyptians share their meet with the less fortunate then gather with their families to celebrate this holy occasion

Christmas

The fun part of celebrating Christmas in Egypt is that you get to celebrate it twice. The traditional Christmas in Egypt is on the 7th of January but Egyptians celebrate Christmas on the 25th of December as well. As a sign of Christmas love, you’d find Christmas decorations all over Egyptian streets and malls from Mid-December till Mid-January. A lot of love is shown to this jolly season.

Easter (Sham El Neseem)

Easter or “Sham El Neseem” is one of the oldest celebrations in Egypt. Egyptians have been celebrating “Sham El Neseem” since Ancient Egypt. “Sham El Neseem” literally means air breathing, but it stands for Easter in Egypt. Egyptians usually celebrate Easter egg coloring with their families and friends then having the traditional Easter meal which is boiled colored eggs and pickled fish. It might not sound as the most delicious dish you can have in Egypt, but it’s very yummy!

Art plays a huge role in the Egyptian culture. Egypt is actually one of the very first civilizations to produce any kind of Art. Music and Dancing are the main forms of art Egyptians are talented in. Oriental Music is always playing in weddings, streets, parties, etc. And Dancing is practically part of every Egyptian boy and girl’s personality, whether it’s normal dancing, Belly dancing, or Tanoura dancing.

Egyptian cuisine is a mix between various cuisines from all over the world but customized to the Egyptian culture and people. Egyptians tend to make good use of meat and vegetables when cooking and that is due to the fact that the Egyptian meat and fruits are ones of the highest quality you can eat. The most famous dishes in the Egyptian cuisine are Ful Medames, Koshary, Molokheya, Shawerma, Kebab, Falafel, and Baklava.

Egypt’s first national dish and one of the tastiest meals you can have in Egypt. The word “Koshary” comes from the French word (couche -de riz) which means “a cover of rice”. It’s a mixture between rice, lentils, and macaroni topped with fried onions and tasty red sauce. It’s a must try dish when you visit Egypt.

Ful medames, or simply fūl, is the second Egyptian national dish of cooked fava beans served with vegetable oil, cumin, with optional addition of chopped parsley, garlic, onion, lemon juice, and chili pepper. Falafel or Taa’meya is the other part of this delicious meal. This dish was originally created in Egypt and later on spread all over the Middle East. You can have this as breakfast or dinner.

A stuffing of rice, seasoned with herbs and spices, put into vegetables like green peppers, aubergines, courgettes, tomatoes, or cabbage leaves. The stuffed vegetable is then cooked with lime and tomato sauce and served later for lunch.

Molokheya is green soup prepared in various styles, where the mallow leaves are very finely chopped, with ingredients such as garlic is added for a characteristic aromatic taste. Egyptians usually have Chicken, Rabbit, or Shrimp as a side protein of the meal with Molokeya. It’s also eaten with Rice or Bread. This is considered as the third national Egyptian dish.

Egypt has many popular deserts such as Basbousa, Kahk (Egyptian Cookies), Baklava, Fateer, Kunafa, Luqmet el Kadi, Qatayef, and Um Ali. All delicious pastries with so much sugar included so get ready for the best sugar rush you’ll ever have!

Rise Up Egypt.

As we witness the entrepreneurial boom in the Egyptian scene, AIESEC decides to plays a proactive role in providing the emerging Start-Ups with young leaders who are capable of growing the Egyptian economy to Rise Up Egypt.

Rise Up Egypt is a project that aims to provide Egyptian Start-Ups with inters from all around the world, who have the capability & motivation to work in various fields needed by the Start-Ups in fields of Management, Marketing, IT, Multimedia production and Project Management.

The inters will get to undergo the entrepreneurial experience with clear responsibility towards growing the business & having an active role to impact the economy overall, develop the professional skills required by their day-to-day job, and get to live the cultural experience of Egypt, where it all begins.

Light Up Egypt

Light Up Egypt is a project that aims to provide Egyptian education centers with interns from all around the world, who have the capability & motivation to teach various subjects & courses to empower Egyptian youth & children with the needed knowledge, skills, perspectives to be more productive change agents in the Egyptian community to be able to develop the country.

The interns undergo an intense educational experience with clear responsibility to impact Egyptian youth & children in order to impact the community overall. The interns will also gain the professional experience required to be able to communicate effectively in an alien work environment, with different audience perspectives, prepare a proper sequential curriculum to build up for the needed output. Moreover, trainees get to live the cultural experience of Egypt, where it all begins.
